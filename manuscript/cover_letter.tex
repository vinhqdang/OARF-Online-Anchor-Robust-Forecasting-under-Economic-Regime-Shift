\bigskip
\noindent We are pleased to submit our manuscript, \textbf{``Online Anchor-Robust
Forecasting under Economic Regime Shift,''} for consideration as a research article in
\textit{Computational Economics}.

Economic and financial time series are punctuated by regime shifts --- geopolitical
shocks, monetary-policy turns, energy crises, regulatory interventions --- and the
dominant response in the online-learning and conformal-prediction literatures is
\emph{reactive}: detect that a change has occurred and then recalibrate. Such methods
are always one step behind and pay a re-adaptation cost at every break. Our paper
develops and evaluates a complementary, \emph{proactive} strategy for the common case
in which shifts travel through an observable channel of regime drivers, such as
geopolitical-risk or policy-uncertainty indices.

The paper makes three contributions. First, we bring \emph{anchor regression} ---
until now a batch estimator --- online, as \textsc{OARF}: a leak-free, first-order
streaming learner that drives the component of its residual correlated with a driver
channel toward zero using past-only exponential-moving-average moments, and that reduces
exactly to online gradient descent when its robustness penalty is switched off. Second,
we propose \emph{interventional regret} as an evaluation lens for online robustness and
prove the fixed-channel learner is no-regret against a switching comparator under
linear structural-causal-model assumptions, with two explicit rates for oracle and
online moment estimation. Third, we provide a careful empirical study --- a controlled
SCM with a held-out intervention grid, plus sixteen years of public daily energy
variance-proxy data --- together with stress tests, assumption-violation scenarios and a
pre-deployment checklist that map precisely \emph{when} proactive anchor robustness
helps and when it fails.

We have tried to be scrupulously honest about scope. The method delivers large
reductions in worst-case interventional error when the anchor is informative and shifts
are anchor-mediated, but we show, and report plainly, that the advantage shrinks or
reverses under noisy, misspecified or non-anchor-mediated shifts; that an exploratory
channel-discovery add-on, while recovering the driver subspace on average, is unstable
and is not covered by our theory; and that on the real energy data all methods lie
within the Model Confidence Set, so \textsc{OARF} is competitive but not statistically
superior. We believe this combination --- a principled streaming method, an
interventional-robustness protocol, and a diagnostic account of its limits --- is a
useful and publishable contribution at the interface of online learning, causal
robustness and economic forecasting, and is well suited to the computational and
methodological focus of \textit{Computational Economics}.

To support full reproducibility, all code, data-build scripts and the exact commands
that regenerate every number, table and figure are publicly available at
\url{https://github.com/vinhqdang/OARF-Online-Anchor-Robust-Forecasting-under-Economic-Regime-Shift}.
